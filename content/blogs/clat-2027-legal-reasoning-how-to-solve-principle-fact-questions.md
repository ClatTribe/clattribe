## What Are Principle-Fact Questions in CLAT?

A Principle-Fact question gives you two components:

**Principle (P):** A legal rule stated in plain English. Accept it as true. No outside legal knowledge required — or allowed.

**Facts (F):** A real-world scenario. Your job is to apply the principle to the facts and reach the conclusion the principle demands.

**Example:**
> **Principle:** A person is liable for negligence if they fail to take reasonable care and that failure directly causes harm to another person.
>
> **Facts:** Rajan parks his car on a slope without engaging the handbrake. The car rolls forward and injures a pedestrian.
>
> **Question:** Is Rajan liable for negligence?

The answer is **yes** — because Rajan failed to take reasonable care (not engaging the handbrake), and that failure directly caused harm. Every element of the principle is satisfied.

Simple in theory. The trap is in the *exceptions*, the *qualifications*, and the deliberate ambiguity that CLAT examiners build into the fact pattern. That's where unprepared aspirants lose marks.

## The 5-Step Method to Solve Any Principle-Fact Question

This is the framework toppers use. Practise it on every question until it runs automatically.

**Step 1 — Read the Principle first, alone.**
Before looking at the facts, understand the principle completely. Ask: Who does it apply to? What condition triggers it? What is the stated outcome?

**Step 2 — Isolate the trigger conditions.**
Every principle has one or more "trigger conditions." For negligence: (a) failure to take reasonable care *and* (b) harm caused. Both must be satisfied. Note them clearly before reading the facts.

**Step 3 — Read the facts with your trigger checklist in hand.**
Go through the fact pattern condition by condition. CLAT examiners deliberately design facts to make one condition *look* present when it isn't — or to hide a qualifying clause you missed.

**Step 4 — Apply, don't interpret.**
This is where most aspirants fail. CLAT does not ask what the law *should* say. It asks what the given principle says. Ignore your personal sense of fairness. Ignore anything you know from IPC, IEA, or CrPC. Apply the principle literally.

**Step 5 — Eliminate before you select.**
CLAT options often include two plausible answers. Eliminate options that use words not supported by the principle — "definitely guilty" when the principle says "may be liable" is a wrong choice. Precision in language is precision in marks.

## Common Mistakes That Drop Your Rank

**Mistake 1: Using real-world legal knowledge.**
If the principle states "a person is guilty of theft if they take another's property without consent," that is the rule for this question — regardless of how Section 378 IPC defines it. Outside knowledge overrides the principle and flips correct answers to wrong ones.

**Mistake 2: Missing exceptions buried in the principle.**
Examiners add clauses like "unless the person had prior consent" or "provided the act was intentional." Missing a single qualifying word reverses the entire answer.

**Mistake 3: Reading facts that aren't there.**
If the facts don't mention consent, don't assume it existed. Answer only from what is stated. Aspirants who imagine additional details fall into every trap the examiner sets.

**Mistake 4: Rushing through the principle.**
Most aspirants spend 10 seconds on the principle and 40 seconds on the facts. Reverse this ratio. The principle is the map. Read it slowly, underline every condition, and only then turn to the scenario.

At the advanced stage, seek out questions with multiple principles. CLAT 2026 introduced passages where two or three legal rules applied to layered facts. The skill is deciding which principle governs — and why.

## FAQs: CLAT 2027 Legal Reasoning Principle-Fact Questions

### Do I need to know actual law to solve Legal Reasoning in CLAT?
No. Each question gives you the principle to apply. Using outside legal knowledge — like what the actual IPC or CrPC says — often produces the wrong answer because the exam principle may differ. Stick to what is given.

### How many Legal Reasoning questions appear in CLAT 2027?
CLAT 2026 contained approximately 28–35 Legal Reasoning questions. CLAT 2027 is expected to follow the same structure, with Principle-Fact questions forming 60–70% of those.

### How much time should I spend per Legal Reasoning question?
Aim for 75–90 seconds. Allocate roughly 20–25 seconds to reading the principle, 30 seconds to the facts, and 20–25 seconds to eliminating answer options. Practise this split from Month 3 onwards.

### Can a Class 11 student start practising Principle-Fact questions now?
Yes — and this is one of the highest-ROI moves a Class 11 student can make. Doing 20 structured questions per week from the start of Class 11 builds pattern recognition that shows up as a 10–12 mark edge by CLAT 2027 exam day.

### How do I handle questions with multiple principles?
Treat each principle separately. Check whether the facts satisfy Principle 1 first, then Principle 2. Answer options in multi-principle questions are often structured around which principle applies — isolating each principle before cross-referencing the facts is essential.

### Is the 5-step method really worth the effort?
Aspirants who use a consistent method score 75–85% in Legal Reasoning. Aspirants relying on instinct score 45–55%. That 20–30 percentage-point gap is roughly 6–9 marks — which, at CLAT's competition level, separates a top-20 NLU seat from falling outside the top 50.
